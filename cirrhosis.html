What is Cirrhosis?

Cirrhosis is a chronic liver disease characterized by the replacement of healthy liver tissue with scar tissue. This scarring disrupts the liver‚Äôs normal structure and function, impairing its ability to process nutrients, hormones, drugs, and toxins, as well as produce proteins and clotting factors. Cirrhosis develops over many years, often as a result of long-term liver damage caused by conditions such as chronic alcohol use, viral hepatitis, or fatty liver disease. If left untreated, cirrhosis can lead to liver failure and other serious complications.

Common Causes of Cirrhosis

  • Chronic hepatitis B or C
  • Fatty liver disease (including non-alcoholic fatty liver)
  • Alcohol-related liver damage
  • Genetic diseases like Cystic Fibrosis or Wilson‚Äôs disease

Symptoms of Cirrhosis

  • Fatigue and weakness
  • Loss of appetite and weight loss
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Jaundice (yellowing of the skin and eyes)
  • Swelling in the legs, feet, or abdomen (edema and ascites)
  • Itchy skin
  • Easy bruising and bleeding
  • Spider-like blood vessels on the skin (spider angiomas)
  • Red palms (palmar erythema)
  • Confusion, difficulty concentrating, or memory problems (hepatic encephalopathy)
  • Dark urine and pale stools
  • Enlarged spleen

Treatments for Cirrhosis

  • Lifestyle changes: Avoid alcohol, maintain a healthy diet, and manage weight.
  • Medications: To control symptoms and complications such as diuretics for fluid buildup, beta-blockers to reduce portal hypertension, and antibiotics to prevent infections.
  • Procedures: Paracentesis (fluid drainage), banding or sclerotherapy to stop bleeding from varices, and TIPS (transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic shunt) to reduce portal vein pressure.
  • Treating underlying causes: Antiviral drugs for hepatitis, managing fatty liver disease, or stopping harmful medications.
  • Liver transplant: In severe cases where liver failure occurs, a transplant may be necessary.
  • Regular monitoring: Frequent check-ups and tests to manage complications early.

Facts About Cirrhosis

  • Cirrhosis is the result of long-term liver damage causing scar tissue to replace healthy liver tissue.
  • Common causes include chronic alcohol use, viral hepatitis, and fatty liver disease.
  • Early cirrhosis may not show symptoms, making it hard to detect until it‚Äôs advanced.
  • Complications include liver failure, portal hypertension, variceal bleeding, and hepatic encephalopathy.
  • Liver transplant is the only cure for end-stage cirrhosis.
  • Healthy lifestyle changes and treating underlying causes can slow progression.
  • Regular medical monitoring is essential for managing symptoms and complications.
